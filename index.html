Purpose Statement Outdoor Planter Box Project

For the first project in Building Industries 003, instructed by Matt Connelly in the Spring of 2026 at Sierra College, the class split into 3 groups to brainstorm.

The project design for a Planter Box for a small garden or flowers outdoors. A local hardwood store donated 4” redwood fence dogear boards for the material.

Each student was given three 1 inch X 5 inch X 6 foot boards.

Ideation and Research

Design

The Group decided on creating a functional 3 plank high box. The aesthetic design centered around a gentle angle for each side to meet at a mitre joint. Thus the base would appear much thinner than the top.

The design process included modleing in Autodesk's Maya to create a visualization of the planter box. Precise measurements were determined and a cut list created for before construction commencement.

Woodwork, Cuts, and Processing

Due to the complex angles, several additional cuts were required join the edges along the different surfaces.

Additionally, warping wood as it dried cause inconsistencies in several phases of the build, glue ups, and post-processing.

Compounding errors led to several inconsitencies. Since the angles are not quite 90deg, the joints were slightly off.

Basic the Step to cut included the following:

  1. Creating the best 3S, or 6S, boards.
  2. Cross cutting the boards to length
  3. Cross cutting the boards to the 7deg gentle angle.
  4. Table Saw Cutting Joining edge 45deg mitre.

    5. This included using a sacrificial plyboard

  5. Cutting the bottom board to fit the joining angle of the planter box.

Usability Testing & Further Development

The final Build in the front of the house.

The alignment was both acute on two angles and obtuse on the other two, leading to an unintended diamond shape.

The 1deg of error, typically adjusted for by careful examination of edges after cutting from the table saw to see if the outter edges pinch or the inner edge bowe.

A key take-away is gluing edges with only two biscuit inserts. The third insert did not assist as intended. On subsequent builds, the less biscuit joints aligned the mitre edges much better.

Summary

Practice, Improve, Review.

Cutting these angles to join at other edges was much more challenging than first inticipated. The brainstorming notes indicate the issue with adjusting the angles for aestetic proposes.

Challenging our skills with new processes improves our overal skill and knowledge base as woodworkers. As a small corrective measure, I created a smaller box without the sloped edges. As seen below.
